# Disruption Response Design

Use this when a new technology, product, or business idea looks unattractive inside the current business but may matter in a different market. The goal is to stop applying sustaining-innovation logic to disruptive change.

## Classify the innovation first

Ask whether the idea is:

- **Sustaining**: improves performance on dimensions current mainstream customers already value.
- **Disruptive**: starts out worse on mainstream metrics, but offers a different value proposition to a different customer set — often simpler, smaller, cheaper, more convenient, or otherwise better for a new or low-end use case.

Decision rules:

- If it helps known customers do what they already want, use the existing optimization logic.
- If current lead customers reject it because it is “not good enough,” treat that as a warning that you may be looking at the wrong value network, not the wrong technology.
- If the required cost structure, margin profile, or channels differ from the core business, assume the mainstream organization will struggle to support it.

## Map the value network, not just the product

Do not evaluate the idea only against the current market.

Check:

- Which customer set values the technology as it exists now?
- Which attributes matter most in that market?
- What gross margins and cost structure are required?
- Would the current business have to sacrifice its main model to compete there?

A technology can be technically simple and still fail in the incumbent value network if it belongs in a different one.

## Find the first market by experiment

For disruptive ideas, market research is usually not enough. The first market often cannot be named in advance.

Use discovery-driven planning:

1. List the assumptions behind the plan.
2. Identify which assumptions must be true first.
3. Test them with the smallest possible real experiments.
4. Preserve flexibility so the plan can change after contact with the market.
5. Keep enough cash, credibility, and time for a second or third try.

Use expeditionary marketing / agnostic marketing:

- Sell or trial real products with real users.
- Watch actual use, not just opinions.
- Look for unexpected applications and buyers.
- Treat focus groups, forecasts, and expert opinion as unreliable when the market does not yet exist.

## Start with a beachhead, not the mainstream

Do not force the first version into the core market.

Instead, look for a small market where the current form of the product is already useful. That beachhead may value the technology’s current limitations as strengths.

Useful tests:

- Does the technology serve a smaller or different customer set today?
- Can it win on convenience, size, simplicity, ruggedness, or low price before it wins on mainstream performance?
- Is the technology improving faster than market needs, so the basis of competition is likely to shift?

Expect the first target market to be wrong at least sometimes. The objective is learning, not perfect prediction.

## Match the organization to the market

If the opportunity is disruptive, do not bury it inside the mainstream unit.

Use a separate setup when the new business needs different:

- customers
- processes
- channels
- metrics
- cost structure
- values

Preferred forms include a spinout, autonomous unit, or other small organization that can treat small early orders as meaningful.

Why this matters:

- Large firms need large near-term growth; small disruptive markets look too small from the core business.
- Middle managers naturally fund projects that fit current quotas, customers, and profit models.
- Mainstream processes and values tend to starve low-margin or uncertain bets.

Rule of thumb: if the project cannot be justified by current profitable customers, expect the mainstream business to veto it implicitly.

## Design for the disruptive use case

Optimize the first version for the market that actually values it now:

- simple
- reliable
- convenient
- low sticker price
- fast iteration
- proven components in a new architecture

Do not make success depend on a breakthrough if the core insight is market fit and organization fit.

Also expect channel conflict:

- mainstream distributors may not care enough to carry the product
- new channels may be necessary
- the disruptive unit may need its own sourcing, sales, and support logic

## Watch for the upmarket move

Once the beachhead works, improve toward adjacent markets.

The pattern to watch is:

- enter a small or low-end market first
- improve faster than customer needs
- move upmarket as performance catches up
- use the beachhead to fund the next step

Do not confuse this with trying to win the top market first. In disruptive settings, the early advantage usually comes from finding the market that values the product as it exists.

## Anti-patterns

Avoid these common mistakes:

- using current lead customers as the sole screen for a disruptive opportunity
- demanding mainstream-scale returns before the market is known
- forcing a low-end or new-market product to share the core business’s cost structure
- assuming a successful sustaining strategy will transfer to disruption
- treating a bad first market guess as proof the venture should die
- keeping the disruptive effort inside the mainstream unit and expecting it to survive resource allocation battles

## Fast diagnostic checklist

- Is this sustaining or disruptive?
- What customer set values it now?
- What attributes matter in that value network?
- Does the current business model block it?
- What small market could buy it first?
- What must we learn before scaling?
- Should this be run as a separate organization?

# People-Centered Influence

## Operating principle
People cooperate more readily when they feel understood, respected, and important. Do not push your agenda harder; make the other person want to move with you.

## Use this when
- you need agreement, help, or a response to a request
- a conversation is becoming defensive or argumentative
- you need to correct someone without creating resentment
- you want to build goodwill quickly and keep it

## Workflow
1. **Pause your own grievance.**
   Ask: *What does the other person want, care about, or protect?*
2. **Begin from their side.**
   Show that you understand their point of view before making your case.
3. **Frame the ask in their benefit.**
   Translate your request into their interests, goals, pride, or sense of usefulness.
4. **Let them participate.**
   Ask for advice, invite their ideas, and let them talk enough to feel ownership.
5. **Protect dignity.**
   Use appreciation, tact, and face-saving language instead of blame, correction, or public defeat.
6. **Close with a concrete next step.**
   Make the action easy, specific, and clearly worthwhile.

## Tactics that consistently help
### Start with appreciation
Use sincere praise, encouragement, and small courtesies. Specific appreciation works; flattery does not.

### Listen more than you speak
Let them explain, repeat key points, and use their name naturally. People respond when they feel heard.

### Get early agreement
Begin with easy points of agreement or simple questions that invite “yes.” Avoid turning the exchange into a contest.

### Make the idea feel like theirs
Plant the idea indirectly, ask what they would suggest, and reflect their judgment back to them. Ownership increases acceptance.

### Appeal to pride carefully
For some people, a challenge works better than pressure. Frame the task as a chance to prove competence or live up to a positive standard.

### Use requests that make them feel useful
When resistance is high, ask for advice, judgment, or help instead of pitching. Keep the request specific, easy, and genuine.

### Correct without humiliation
If you must point out an error:
- begin with what was done well
- use suggestion or questions instead of blunt accusation
- admit your own mistakes when relevant
- make the correction feel easy and worthwhile
- help the person save face

## Rules to avoid resistance
- Do not criticize first if you want cooperation.
- Do not tell people they are wrong.
- Do not argue to win the point.
- Do not use insincere praise.
- Do not make the person feel cornered, diminished, or talked down to.

## Practical phrasing
- “I may be wrong, but could we look at it this way?”
- “You may be right.”
- “What would you suggest?”
- “Tell me more about that.”
- “I appreciate the way you handled that.”
- “Would you mind helping me with this?”
- “Have you considered…?”

## Quick checks
- If the message feels like a grievance, rewrite it as a benefit to them.
- If they are defensive, slow down and listen before explaining.
- If they are important to the outcome, make them feel useful and respected.
- If you want lasting goodwill, pair requests with genuine appreciation and small acts of courtesy.

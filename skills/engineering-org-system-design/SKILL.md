# Engineering Org System Design

Treat the engineering org as a system you can model, tune, and stabilize. Use this skill when headcount, structure, priorities, or cross-team work are becoming hard to reason about.

## Core operating frame
- Diagnose the constraint before acting.
- Prefer durable system changes over heroic short-term fixes.
- Concentrate attention and resources where they can actually stabilize the system.
- Keep decisions explicit: what problem, what policy, what action.

## Design teams and spans to fit the work
- Keep manager spans around 6–8 engineers in steady state.
- Keep managers-of-managers around 4–6 direct reports.
- Avoid tiny teams; teams under 4 people are usually fragile.
- Do not leave managers with more than 8 direct reports.
- Grow a team to about 8–10, then split into two teams of 4–5.
- Never create empty teams.
- Prefer keeping innovation and maintenance together unless there is a strong reason not to.
- When headcount changes, design from the boxes, not from the current people.

## Diagnose team state before choosing help
Use a simple four-state model:
- **Falling behind**: backlog grows, morale and user satisfaction are low.
- **Treading water**: critical work gets done, but debt and new bets stall.
- **Repaying debt**: debt paydown starts compounding.
- **Innovating**: low debt, high morale, mostly new value work.

Response rules:
- Falling behind -> add net-new people and manage expectations.
- Treading water -> reduce WIP and consolidate work.
- Repaying debt -> protect time so the compounding payoff can happen.
- Innovating -> preserve slack so the team does not get defunded.

## Change the system, not just the symptoms
- Do not peanut-butter people across all teams.
- Shift scope before shifting people.
- If you must redeploy people, use temporary rotations with fixed duration.
- Keep slack in the system; it makes throughput and bottlenecks easier to reason about.
- Expect teams to need time to gel after growth or change.

## Run migrations in three phases
### 1) De-risk
- Write the design early.
- Start with the hardest teams and edge cases.
- Work side-by-side with the hardest 1–2 teams.
- Do not start with the easy cases; they create false confidence.

### 2) Enable
- Build tooling for the easy 90%.
- Provide self-service docs and incremental, reversible tools.
- Treat docs and tools like products: watch where teams get stuck and revise.

### 3) Finish
- Stop new usage of the old way first.
- Track the long tail and push status through managers.
- Be willing to finish awkward edge cases yourself.
- Celebrate completion, not just the start of the work.

## Reorganize only for structural reasons
Before a reorg, ask:
- Is the problem structural?
- Am I avoiding a relationship issue?
- Does the problem already exist?
- Are conditions temporary?

Rules:
- The best reorg is the one you do not do.
- Use reorgs to solve structure, not to dodge management problems.
- Project headcount a year out.
- Plan teams, managers, staffing, and interfaces from the boxes outward.
- Roll out with reasoning, impact notes, and direct support for the most affected people.

## Use policy and metrics to scale judgment
- Write opinionated policy that constrains behavior.
- Treat repeated exceptions as exception debt.
- Batch escalations and revisit policy on a future date.
- If constraints do not really constrain, write a norm instead of weak policy.
- Use metrics with target, baseline, trend, and time frame.
- Pair investment goals with baseline goals so one success does not create a new problem.
- For change with limited authority, explore, dive, attribute, contextualize, nudge, baseline, and review.

## Triage incoming asks
For every request, choose exactly one:
- **Close out**: finish this specific ask completely.
- **Solve**: remove the class of ask for about six months.
- **Delegate**: route it to the right owner or skill.

Do not invent a fourth path. This keeps you from accumulating ambiguous work.

## Keep hiring, performance, and leadership systems coherent
- Treat hiring as an organizational system, not a one-off event.
- Instrument the funnel, then improve the weakest stage.
- Use structured rubrics and explicit role models rather than committee intuition.
- Keep performance ladders short and self-contained.
- Watch for level drift and gate openings when top levels expand.
- Use consistent calibration and address poor performance immediately.
- Create specialized roles only when the recognition gap is real, the sponsor exists, and the role has a self-sustaining mission plus a ladder from day one.

## Watch for common failure modes
- Heroics that hide broken policy.
- Over-optimizing locally while the org degrades globally.
- Disassembling healthy teams just to chase priorities.
- Delegating unsolved, hard-to-fix risk to healthy teams.
- Running on exceptions instead of policy.
- Confusing more motion with more progress.

## Working standard
When stuck, ask: what system is producing this pattern, what constraint is real, and what durable change would make the next six months easier? Then choose the smallest structural fix that can actually hold.

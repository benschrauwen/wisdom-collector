# Repair Team Dysfunction

## When to use
Use this when a team looks busy but is not acting like a team: meetings are overly polite or tense, people stay silent, decisions linger, peers avoid holding each other accountable, or functional interests outrank shared outcomes.

## Operating model
The dysfunctions usually stack in order:
1. absence of trust
2. fear of conflict
3. lack of commitment
4. avoidance of accountability
5. inattention to results

Fix the earlier link before expecting the later one to improve.

## Diagnose before intervening
- Observe meetings and informal behavior before trying to fix anything.
- Say the problem plainly: the team is not functioning as a team.
- Use a shared diagnostic check on the five dysfunctions and discuss the gaps together.
- Treat 8–9 as mostly healthy, 6–7 as watchful, and 3–5 as needing action.

## Reset the team’s rules
- Be present.
- Participate.
- No hidden side work during discussion.
- Say when a topic should be handled offline.
- Put important work in the room, not in email.
- If a meeting has nothing worth debating, cancel it.

## Build trust through vulnerability
Trust means teammates can admit weaknesses, mistakes, skill gaps, and requests for help without fear of reprisal.

Practical moves:
- Start with low-risk personal history sharing to humanize people.
- Move to concrete strengths and weaknesses as they affect team success.
- Ask teammates to challenge self-assessments respectfully.
- Use feedback for development, not as a hidden performance weapon.
- Go first as the leader; do not ask for vulnerability you will not model.

## Turn tension into productive conflict
Healthy teams debate ideas hard and recover quickly. Bad teams replace conflict with silence, sarcasm, politics, or back-channel complaints.

How to drive it:
- Ask for objections and dissent explicitly.
- Keep people in the room until real differences are surfaced.
- Appoint someone to mine for buried disagreement.
- Remind the group that conflict about ideas is necessary and useful.
- Let disagreement run long enough to resolve the issue instead of cutting it off for comfort.

## Force commitment after the debate
Commitment means clarity plus buy-in, not unanimity.

Do this:
- Close each decision explicitly.
- Make sure everyone has had a chance to weigh in.
- Summarize the decision, the target, and who will communicate what.
- Set real deadlines and honor them.
- If debate stalls, narrow it to one decision and force closure.
- Use “disagree and commit” once the decision is made.

## Make peers accountable
Peer accountability is the team’s main enforcement mechanism; the leader should not be the only disciplinarian.

Do this:
- Publish goals and standards where everyone can see them.
- Review progress in the room, not only in private.
- Ask peers directly about missed commitments.
- Push with respect and assume positive intent, but do not hold back.
- Call out behavior that hurts the team even when the person is valuable or influential.

## Lock onto shared results
Refocus the team on collective outcomes, not status, politics, or individual agendas.

Do this:
- Define a simple scoreboard of shared results.
- Track it frequently enough to correct course early.
- Make everyone responsible for all core outcomes, not just their own function.
- Tie recognition and rewards to team results.
- Ask whether a person is helping the team win or mainly advancing themselves.

## Keep the repair process honest
- Expect discomfort; the team may get worse before it gets better.
- Be willing to confront destructive behavior face to face.
- If someone is actively misaligned and cannot be redirected, be prepared to remove them.
- Watch for progress signs: more animation, more laughter, more follow-up, and less splintering.
- Persist; behavioral change is usually slower than the team wants.

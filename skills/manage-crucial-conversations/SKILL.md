# Manage Crucial Conversations

Use this when a conversation has high stakes, differing opinions, and strong emotions, and you need to keep meaning flowing instead of sliding into silence or violence.

## Core sequence
1. **Start with heart** — manage your motives.
2. **Learn to look** — notice when safety is dropping.
3. **Make it safe** — repair purpose and respect before pushing content.
4. **State your path** — share your view tentatively and invite testing.
5. **Explore their path** — make it safe for others to speak honestly.
6. **Move to action** — decide how to decide, assign follow-through, and document commitments.

## Start with heart
Work on yourself first. Ask:
- What do I really want for me?
- What do I really want for others?
- What do I really want for the relationship?
- How would I behave if I really wanted those results?

Reset when your motive drifts toward winning, saving face, revenge, or staying safe by going silent.

Reject **Sucker’s Choice** thinking. Do not accept false either/or options like honest or kind, speak up or stay safe, win or lose. Reframe the problem as an **and** question:
- Clarify what you want.
- Clarify what you do not want.
- Ask how you can get both.

## Learn to look
Watch both:
- **content** — what is being said
- **conditions** — what is happening to people and the relationship

Treat these as safety alarms:
- silence, withdrawal, vagueness, sarcasm, sugarcoating
- controlling, labeling, attacking, dominating
- tension, defensiveness, circular arguments, repeated shutdowns

Do not mirror aggression or withdrawal. Read it as a sign that safety is at risk.

## Make it safe
When safety drops, step out of the content and repair the conditions first.

Check whether the problem is about:
- **Mutual Purpose** — do they believe you care about their goals and motives?
- **Mutual Respect** — do they believe you respect them as a person?

Repair tools:
- **Contrasting** — say what you do **not** mean and what you **do** mean when your words are being misunderstood.
- **Apologize** — acknowledge your role and express sincere sorrow when you have clearly caused harm.
- **CRIB** — when you are truly at cross-purposes:
  - **C**ommit to seek Mutual Purpose
  - **R**ecognize the purpose behind the strategy
  - **I**nvent a Mutual Purpose
  - **B**rainstorm new strategies

## State your path
When you need to share risky meaning, do it in this order:
1. **Facts first** — start with observable, specific, verifiable behavior.
2. **Tell your story as a story** — use tentative language: “I’m beginning to wonder if…”, “Maybe…”, “It seems like…”.
3. **Invite others to share their path** — ask for their facts, stories, and feelings.
4. **Encourage testing** — make dissent safe: “What am I missing here?”

Aim for candid and tentative, not soft and vague and not hard and certain.

## Explore their path
When others blow up or clam up, respond with curiosity and patience.

Sequence:
- **Ask** — invite them to share
- **Mirror** — reflect the emotion or behavior you see
- **Paraphrase** — restate their view in your own words
- **Prime** — offer your best guess only if they still hold back

Rules:
- stay calm; your tone creates safety
- do not pretend to listen
- understanding is not agreement
- if you have listened enough and are stuck, back off rather than pester

When responding, use **Agree / Build / Compare**:
- **Agree** when you agree
- **Build** when they left out an important piece
- **Compare** when you differ; describe your path as different, not wrong

## Move to action
Dialogue and decision-making are not the same thing. Before ending the conversation, decide how the decision will be made.

Choose the decision method deliberately:
- **Command** — one person decides
- **Consult** — gather input, then decide
- **Vote** — choose among acceptable options when speed matters
- **Consensus** — everyone sincerely supports one decision

Use the method that matches who cares, who knows, who must agree, and how many people it is worth involving.

Then close the loop:
- specify **who** is responsible
- specify **what** they will do
- specify **by when**
- specify **how follow-up happens**
- record conclusions, decisions, and assignments
- review them at the next check-in

Treat missed promises as dialogue problems, not just performance problems.

## Quick prompts
- What do I really want for me, others, and the relationship?
- What am I pretending not to notice?
- Why would a reasonable, rational, decent person do that?
- What do I not mean, and what do I do mean?
- What am I missing here?
- Who does what by when?

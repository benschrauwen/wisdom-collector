# Run transparent OKRs

Use this when you need a practical operating system for focus, alignment, accountability, and stretch. The core move is to define a small number of objectives, attach measurable key results, make them visible, and review them on a regular cadence.

## Write OKRs correctly

- **Objective = what** you want to achieve.
  - Make it significant, concrete, and action-oriented.
  - Keep it short and easy to understand.
- **Key Results = how** you will know it is happening.
  - Make them specific, measurable, time-bound, and verifiable.
  - If all KRs are met, the objective should be achieved.
  - If not, the OKR was probably too vague or incomplete.

### Quick test

If a KR reads like an activity word — such as *consult*, *analyze*, or *participate* — rewrite it as an outcome.

## Keep the set small

- Limit to **3–5 objectives per cycle**.
- Use **5 or fewer key results per objective**.
- Less is more: too many goals dilute focus and weaken accountability.

## Balance direction and ownership

- Make goals **public** whenever possible.
- Start with **leadership commitment**; people judge the system by whether leaders use it.
- Use a **mixed model**:
  - some top-down objectives for shared direction,
  - some bottom-up objectives for local insight and ownership.
- Keep **single ownership** for each OKR.
  - Others can link to it.
  - Avoid shared ownership if accountability matters.
- Make dependencies explicit and update affected teams when goals change.

## Choose the right kind of goal

### Committed goals

Use when the outcome must be delivered in full.

- Treat anything below full completion as failure.
- Use for operational reliability and must-hit work.
- Score these strictly.

### Stretch goals

Use when you want ambition, learning, or breakthrough performance.

- Aim high enough that success may land around **60–70%**.
- Keep them specific, measurable, and directional.
- Do not use stretch goals as vague aspiration.
- Leaders must communicate both importance and belief; otherwise teams disengage.

### Rule of thumb

If the goal is supposed to be reliable, do not score it like a stretch goal. If it is supposed to be a moonshot, do not punish partial attainment the same way you would for a committed objective.

## Track OKRs as living work

- Use a visible, current system rather than static documents.
- Review progress regularly — weekly, monthly, or by team need.
- Prevent “zombie OKRs” by updating, starting, or stopping goals mid-cycle when conditions change.
- If you drop an OKR, notify anyone who depends on it.

### Status questions

- **Continue**: Is it on track?
- **Update**: Did the environment or workflow change?
- **Start**: Is a new need more important now?
- **Stop**: Is this no longer useful?

## Review and learn every cycle

At the end of the cycle, do three things:

1. **Score the objective**
2. **Self-assess**
3. **Reflect**

Use the score as input for learning, not just judgment. Add context such as effort, market conditions, luck, and quality of outcome.

### Useful reflection prompts

- Did I accomplish the objective?
- What helped or blocked progress?
- What would I change next cycle?
- What should carry forward into future OKRs?

## Guardrails

- Do not tie OKRs directly to bonuses or performance punishment.
- Do not let a goal be met by harming safety, ethics, quality, or reputation; pair output metrics with quality or counter-metrics.
- Do not make every goal top-down.
- Do not hide weak goals behind ambiguous language or a sea of yellow status.
- Do not let cadence slip so far that check-ins become stale annual paperwork.

## Use manager check-ins to keep the system alive

In recurring 1:1s and progress reviews:

- Ask what is working well.
- Ask what is not working well.
- Ask what is blocked.
- Ask what help is needed.
- Ask what should start, stop, or change.

The manager’s job is to help the person achieve the key results, not to turn the meeting into inspection theater. Let the employee do most of the talking.

## Culture and rollout

If the organization does not have trust, clarity, or accountability, fix that first or OKRs will become cosmetic.

Healthy rollout patterns:

- Start with leadership.
- Make goals public and repeat the rationale until people can explain it back.
- Use real-time feedback and recognition alongside OKRs.
- Treat transparency as a working norm, not a slogan.
- Expect several quarterly cycles before the system matures.

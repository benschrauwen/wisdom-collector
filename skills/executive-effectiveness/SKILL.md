# Executive Effectiveness

Use this when work is getting crowded out by urgency, meetings, internal pressure, or activity that does not change results. The goal is to move from being busy to producing results.

## Operating principle
Effectiveness is a learned discipline, not a personality trait. Treat time as the scarcest resource, then work through five questions:
1. Where does time actually go?
2. What contribution is expected of me?
3. Which strengths should I build on?
4. What few priorities are worth protecting?
5. What decision will actually get executed and tested?

## 1) Start with time, not tasks
Begin with an actual time record, not a wish list.

### Audit questions
- What would happen if this were not done at all?
- Can this be delegated, refused, or eliminated?
- What am I doing that wastes other people’s time?
- Which recurring crises are really symptoms of bad design or poor foresight?
- Is the organization overstaffed, malorganized, or sending information in the wrong form or too late?

### Rules of thumb
- Expect to remove a meaningful share of time demands without visible damage.
- Protect large uninterrupted blocks for major work; small gaps are usually useless.
- Repeated emergencies should be converted into routine or prevented.
- If you cut too much, the imbalance will show quickly.

## 2) Define contribution in outward terms
Ask: **What can I contribute that will significantly affect the results of the institution?**

Contribution includes:
- direct results
- building and reaffirming values
- developing people who will carry the work forward

### Use this test before acting
- Who will use my output?
- What do they need to know or decide?
- In what form, and by when, do they need it?
- What contribution from me do they require to make their contribution effective?

If you are focused mainly on your own authority, specialty, or effort, you are acting like a subordinate. Focus on contribution and results.

## 3) Staff and work from strengths
Do not build around weakness. Build around what a person can do exceptionally well for a specific task.

### Appointment and promotion
Before assigning a role, ask:
- Does this person have one major area of strength?
- Is that strength relevant to this task?
- If they excel there, will it make a significant difference?

Choose the best performer for the job, not the least objectionable person.

### Guardrails
- A weakness is usually a limitation unless it involves integrity or character.
- “Indispensable” is usually a warning sign, not praise.
- If several capable people fail in the same job, redesign the job before hunting for genius.
- Two mediocrities do not add up to one strong performer.

### Manage upward as well as downward
Do not try to fix a boss’s weaknesses. Instead:
- identify what they do well
- present issues in a form they can use
- match their preferred mode: summary vs detail, reader vs listener, early involvement vs late
- make their strengths productive and their weaknesses irrelevant

### Use your own working style
Stop copying an image of how an executive is supposed to work. Build on how you actually do your best work:
- morning or night
- prepared text or rough notes
- solo work or committee work
- fast drafting or meticulous drafting
- pressure or ample time

## 4) Concentrate on the few things that matter
Do first things first, and one thing at a time.

### Priority rules
- Choose the future over the past.
- Choose opportunity over problem.
- Choose your own direction over the bandwagon.
- Aim high enough to matter.
- Treat yesterday’s successes as temporary unless they still produce.

### Use posteriorities, not just priorities
It is not enough to rank the important work. You must also refuse to keep carrying the unimportant work.

Before starting something new, ask:
- If we did not already do this, would we start it now?
- What old work must be dropped to free people for the new work?

### Practical concentration habits
- Reserve continuous time before defending it.
- Batch meetings and interruptions when possible.
- Keep high-energy periods for major work.
- Do not let urgent demands decide your agenda.

## 5) Make decisions that can execute and learn
Treat decisions as judgments between alternatives, not as a search for perfect certainty.

### Decision sequence
1. Define the real decision.
2. State the alternatives.
3. Identify the right measure of relevance.
4. Invite disagreement and test the strongest opposing case.
5. Specify execution.
6. Build in feedback.

### Good decision rules
- Do not decide by acclamation.
- Use dissent to surface blind spots and alternatives.
- Ask what the other side must be seeing for its view to make sense.
- Do not make half-measures; if you act, act decisively enough to solve the problem.
- If nothing will change by waiting, do not force a decision.

### Build execution in before you commit
A decision is incomplete until you know:
- what actions follow
- who is responsible
- what people are available to carry it out

### Build feedback in before you commit
- Define how the decision will be tested against reality.
- Go and look for yourself when the situation matters.
- Recheck assumptions when distance from the action increases.
- Treat a good decision as temporary unless reality keeps confirming it.

## 6) Use courage to close the loop
Once analysis is sufficient, the main obstacle is often not insight but courage.

Do not hide behind another study unless new information is likely to matter. If a nagging uneasiness remains, pause briefly, inspect the issue, then decide.

The standard is simple: get the right things done.

## Anti-patterns to avoid
- confusing activity with effectiveness
- letting inside-organizational visibility dominate outside results
- filling the day with small driblets of time
- staffing for general goodness instead of task-specific strength
- making important decisions without disagreement, execution, or feedback
- preserving yesterday’s work because it is familiar

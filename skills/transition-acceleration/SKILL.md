# Transition acceleration

Use this skill when someone is entering a new leadership role and needs to ramp up quickly without relying on an old success formula.

## Core aim
Reach the **breakeven point** as early as possible: contribute more value than the transition consumes in time, attention, and political capital.

## Operating sequence

### 1) Mentally leave the old role
- Create a clear breakpoint if the move is messy.
- Consciously let go of old responsibilities.
- Redefine how the new role differs in thinking, influence, and behavior.
- Watch for strengths becoming liabilities, such as detail orientation turning into micromanagement or technical confidence turning into overreach.

### 2) Build a learning agenda before acting
Learn what matters before trying to fix it.

Organize questions into three time horizons:
- **Past:** how the situation got here, what has worked, what has failed.
- **Present:** strategy, people, processes, land mines, early wins.
- **Future:** challenges, opportunities, barriers, resources, and what should be preserved vs. changed.

Use structured sources, not just ad hoc conversations:
- direct reports, peers, boss, customers, suppliers, staff functions
- historical data, process reviews, tours, surveys, and focused one-on-ones

A strong loop is:
1. gather data from multiple sources
2. compare answers across people
3. extract actionable insights
4. revisit the agenda as understanding deepens

### 3) Diagnose the situation before choosing strategy
Classify the assignment with **STARS**:
- **Start-up:** build from scratch
- **Turnaround:** rescue a troubled unit
- **Realignment:** revive a drifting but viable business
- **Sustaining success:** protect a strong business and take it higher

Also check culture load across:
- organizational culture
- professional/functional culture
- geographic culture

If the cultural shift is large, spend more energy learning and adapting before trying to change things.

### 4) Match early moves to the situation
Use the right bias for the job:
- **Start-up / turnaround:** bias toward doing, speed, and decisive calls.
- **Realignment / sustaining success:** bias toward learning, coalition-building, and cultural diagnosis.
- **Turnaround:** defend the core first, then go on offense.
- **Sustaining success:** earn the right to change what worked before changing it.

Choose early wins that fit the situation:
- get the right team and focus in a start-up
- show visible progress cutting back in a turnaround
- build urgency in a realignment
- signal respect for past success in sustaining success

Keep early wins focused on **2–3 priorities max**. They should do double duty:
- build credibility now
- support long-term goals and desired behavior change

### 5) Negotiate success with the boss early
Treat the boss relationship as a working contract.

Hold five conversations:
- **Situation:** shared diagnosis and constraints
- **Expectations:** goals, timing, measures of success
- **Style:** communication and decision preferences
- **Resources:** authority, people, time, political backing
- **Development:** how the role should stretch you

Rules:
- do not surprise the boss
- do not trash the past
- bring a point of view, not just problems
- underpromise and overdeliver
- adapt to the boss’s style without surrendering results

If expectations or resources are unclear, clarify them in writing and reset them early.

### 6) Align the organization before redesigning it
Think in terms of **strategy, structure, systems, skills, and culture**.

Start with strategy:
- Which customers, capabilities, and commitments matter?
- Is the strategy coherent, adequate, and actually implemented?

Then inspect the rest:
- structure that blocks flow or decision rights
- systems that reward the wrong behavior
- skills gaps or underused expertise
- culture assumptions about power and value

Avoid the usual traps:
- restructuring before diagnosing root causes
- automating broken processes
- changing things for their own sake
- overestimating how much change the group can absorb

### 7) Build the right team and coalition
First get the right people in the right jobs.

Assess team members with a fixed set of criteria:
- competence
- judgment
- energy
- focus
- relationships
- trust

Sort people into action categories:
- keep
- keep and develop
- move
- observe
- replace

Then align goals, incentives, and meeting processes so the team can execute.

Do not stop at the vertical chain. Build horizontal support early:
- peers
- customers and suppliers
- internal influencers
- natural historians and culture interpreters

Map the informal network before trying to move it. Identify supporters, opponents, and swing voters, then sequence your influence accordingly.

### 8) Protect your balance
Transitions fail when leaders become brittle, isolated, or overextended.

Install a few disciplines:
- weekly plan / evaluate / plan again
- protect time for hard thinking
- step back before reacting
- defer commitment when needed
- reflect on what feels off, what is working, and where you are avoiding tough calls

Build support systems at work and at home. A stable home front, a trusted advice network, and clear routines make it easier to sustain good judgment.

## Anti-patterns
- Acting on assumptions before learning
- Reusing an old playbook without diagnosis
- Trying to change everything at once
- Managing only upward and downward while ignoring peers and informal power
- Forcing closure before the balance of forces supports it
- Letting early stress make you rigid, defensive, or isolated

## Quick prompts
- What made me successful before, and what no longer fits?
- What do I need to learn before I act?
- What kind of situation is this, really?
- What should I preserve, and what should I change?
- What does my boss expect, and what support do I need?
- Who has influence here, and who needs to be brought along?

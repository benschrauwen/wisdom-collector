# Strategic Value-Based Negotiation

Use this when a deal depends on more than price alone, when hidden stakeholders or deadlines matter, or when the conversation is slipping into positions, pressure, or personality conflict.

## Core stance
- Do not bargain over positions by default.
- Be hard on the merits, soft on the people.
- Separate the people from the problem.
- Focus on interests, not positions.
- Use objective criteria instead of will contests.
- Compare every offer to your BATNA; do not rely on an arbitrary bottom line.

## Prepare like the negotiation depends on it
- Map your interests, their interests, shared interests, and likely tradeoffs.
- Identify hidden stakeholders, constituencies, blockers, and decision-makers.
- Clarify deadlines, constraints, risks, and what happens if no deal is reached.
- Build your BATNA and test the other side’s claimed alternatives.
- Define a trip wire: a package that is still better than your BATNA but tells you to pause or reassess.
- Draft the standards, arguments, and precedents you can justify.
- If the final deal will need writing, sketch a framework agreement early.

## Run the conversation
### Separate people from the problem
- Treat the people as human beings; treat the issue as the shared target.
- Diagnose the people side in three buckets: perception, emotion, and communication.
- Do not solve a people problem by conceding on substance.
- Put yourself in their shoes.
- Do not infer intent from your fears.
- Do not blame them for your problem.
- Discuss perceptions explicitly and without accusation.
- Make emotion explicit and legitimate.
- Let the other side let off steam; listen quietly and do not react to outbursts.
- Listen actively, repeat back what you heard, and speak so you can be understood.
- Speak about your concerns and feelings, not their motives.
- Speak with purpose; do not reveal flexibility or information without a reason.

### Focus on interests, not positions
- Ask why each position matters.
- Ask why not, and what consequences they fear.
- Explain your interests explicitly; do not assume they will infer them.
- Make interests concrete and easy to picture.
- Acknowledge their interests before pressing yours.
- Keep the discussion forward-looking.
- Look for basic needs underneath the stated demand: security, economic well-being, belonging, recognition, and control.

### Invent options for mutual gain
- Separate inventing from deciding.
- Generate many options before judging any of them.
- Look for low-cost-to-you / high-benefit-to-them trades.
- Look for differences in time, risk, form, scope, and certainty.
- Use side-by-side brainstorming when it helps:
  - no criticism during idea generation
  - record everything visibly
  - keep the group small and informal
  - revisit and improve promising ideas later
- Search for shared interests and make them explicit.
- When talks stall, use joint drafting or one-text style process moves to keep the focus on the package instead of the positions.
- Turn proposals into a yesable proposition: concrete, implementable, and easy to justify.

### Insist on objective criteria
- Turn the discussion into a joint search for fair standards.
- Ask for the theory behind their proposal.
- Agree on the standard first, then apply it.
- Use standards that are legitimate, practical, independent of either side’s will, and reciprocally applicable.
- Common standards include market value, precedent, professional judgment, equal treatment, efficiency, reciprocity, and similar norms.
- If substantive criteria are disputed, use a fair procedure instead.
- Never yield to pressure alone; yield only to principle.

## Use BATNA as the real bottom line
- Do not lock yourself to an arbitrary bottom line.
- Improve your alternatives early.
- Compare every offer to your best alternative, not to your hopes.
- A weak or unknown BATNA makes you either too accommodating or too timid.
- Use your BATNA to decide whether negotiation makes sense at all.
- If exiting, leave in a way that preserves the possibility of return.

## Move from talk to commitment
- Draft as you go when the deal will need writing.
- Build a framework agreement if details still need to be filled in.
- Move in stages: consensus option, narrowed range, then commitment.
- Make commitments explicit, tentative while exploring, and concrete when final.
- Ask how each side will justify the deal to its constituents.
- End generously when a final concession will improve implementation or future talks.

## If pressure or manipulation shows up
- Recognize it, name it, question it, and return to interests, standards, and process.
- Do not answer pressure with pressure or with a lesson-teaching posture.
- If the other side is using positional tactics, change the game instead of fighting on their terms.

## Quick self-check
- Would I use this with a friend or family member?
- Would I be embarrassed to see the exchange in print?
- Does this fit the kind of negotiation I want to be known for?

# Resonant leadership

Use this when group performance depends on changing the emotional climate, not just improving the plan.

## Core operating loop
1. Read the room.
2. Regulate yourself.
3. Choose the leadership style that fits the moment.
4. Use empathy and feedback to stay accurate.
5. If the climate is toxic, surface the hidden norms and reset the system.

## Read the room
Look for signs of:
- **Resonance**: engagement, laughter, cooperation, energy, open eyes
- **Dissonance**: silence, fear, anger, withdrawal, stonewalling, apathy

Ask:
- What are people feeling right now?
- What meaning are they making from events?
- What mood am I transmitting through tone, face, timing, and attention?

## Regulate yourself first
- Do not vent distress publicly.
- If frustration, rage, anxiety, or panic spikes, pause until reasoning returns.
- Check whether your actions fit your values; transparency without impulse control is not authenticity.
- Use calm, upbeat, confident energy to create trust.
- Treat intuition as learned pattern recognition: weigh the data, then check whether the choice feels right. If it does not, slow down or rethink.

## Choose the right style for the situation
Switch styles fluidly instead of defaulting to one habit.

- **Visionary**: when the group needs a shared direction or purpose.
- **Coaching**: when a person needs long-term development.
- **Affiliative**: when you need to heal rifts, rebuild morale, or support people under stress.
- **Democratic**: when you need buy-in, ideas, or consensus and people have relevant knowledge.
- **Pacesetting**: only with highly competent, motivated teams that need a quick high bar.
- **Commanding**: only in crisis, urgent turnaround, emergency, or chronic problem behavior; use briefly and with respect.

Decision rule:
- change or vision → visionary
- development → coaching
- trust repair or harmony → affiliative
- buy-in or ideas → democratic
- quick excellence → pacesetting, sparingly
- emergency or turnaround → commanding, briefly

## Keep trust intact
- Read faces, voice, and context, then tune your response.
- Take feelings into account without being ruled by them.
- If you are disingenuous or manipulative, people will sense it.
- Ask for candid, multi-source feedback to avoid CEO disease and self-deception.

## Guardrails
- Do not confuse positivity with denial.
- Do not use fear, coercion, or polished insincerity as substitutes for resonance.
- Do not overuse pacesetting or commanding; they can create anxiety, silence, and turnover.
- Do not rely on one-off workshops or praise; lasting change needs repetition, feedback, and relationships.

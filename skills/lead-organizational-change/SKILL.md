# Lead organizational change

Use this when a major change is stalling, fragmenting into disconnected projects, or getting blocked by politics, structure, or old habits. The core move is to lead the change as a sequence, not manage it as a single plan.

## Operating model
Major change is a multistage process:

1. Establish urgency
2. Create a guiding coalition
3. Develop vision and strategy
4. Communicate the vision
5. Empower broad-based action
6. Generate short-term wins
7. Consolidate gains and produce more change
8. Anchor new approaches in culture

Sequence matters. If urgency is weak or the coalition is thin, later execution usually turns into resistance, delay, or symbolic activity.

## Lead it, don’t just manage it
- Use **leadership** to set direction, align people, and create momentum.
- Use **management** to budget, plan, organize, and control the work.
- For major transformation, leadership has to dominate; management alone tends to preserve the status quo.
- Treat the effort as **many projects within projects**. Different parts may move at different speeds, but they still need a shared direction.

## 1) Establish urgency
If people think “we’re fine,” nothing else will stick.

Raise urgency by:
- exposing managers to concrete competitive weaknesses and customer pain
- increasing direct contact with dissatisfied customers, suppliers, or shareholders
- raising standards and targets sharply
- making more people accountable for broad business results
- using outside parties when insiders filter bad news
- removing signals of excess and comfort

Watch for complacency signals:
- no visible crisis
- past success creating comfort
- silo thinking hiding the real problem
- internal metrics that reward narrow goals
- polished optimism from the top that neutralizes concern

## 2) Build a guiding coalition
Do not rely on a lone leader or a low-credibility committee.

A workable coalition needs:
- **position power**: enough key players to prevent easy blocking
- **expertise**: the right perspectives and experience
- **credibility**: people others respect
- **leadership**: enough people who can actually drive change
- **trust and shared purpose**: not just attendance

Avoid:
- **big egos** that dominate the room and subordinate the goal
- **snakes** that spread mistrust and triangulate
- **reluctant central players** who refuse to commit unless forced; if they are central and toxic, address them directly

Build trust with repeated joint work, open discussion, mixed assignments, and off-site meetings that combine analysis with honest interaction. If the group does not develop mutual respect and a common goal, it is not yet a coalition.

## 3) Develop vision and strategy
A useful vision is a **picture of the future plus a reason to strive for it**.

Make it:
- imaginable
- desirable to the main stakeholders
- feasible, not fantasy
- focused enough to guide decisions
- flexible enough to adapt
- short enough to explain in five minutes or less

Use strategy to explain how the vision becomes real.

Avoid visions that are:
- vague slogans
- thick binders
- purely financial targets
- average-benefit compromises that excite no one
- biased to one constituency at the expense of others

Create the first draft, then shape it with the coalition. Expect an iterative process that takes time and engages both head and heart.

## 4) Communicate the vision
Communicate until people can repeat it back and act on it.

Use these rules:
- keep it simple
- use metaphors, analogies, and concrete examples
- repeat it relentlessly through many forums
- lead by example
- explain inconsistencies instead of letting them fester
- listen and let others push back

If the vision cannot be explained in five minutes and still create interest, it is not ready.

Do not let leaders say one thing and do another; behavior destroys credibility faster than silence.

## 5) Empower broad-based action
Empowerment means removing barriers to action, not giving a speech about empowerment.

Remove:
- structural barriers such as silos, extra layers, or fragmented authority
- system barriers in appraisal, compensation, promotion, hiring, planning, or information systems
- skill gaps in technical, team, social, or role-transition skills
- supervisor barriers from managers who keep old norms alive

Confront blockers early. A single supervisor can neutralize a broad change effort if left alone.

When you find a blocker, use honest dialogue:
- state the vision and the timetable
- say what help is needed
- ask what can be done to help them help the change
- determine whether they can support the effort or need to be replaced

### Train for the actual change
Training must match the new behavior, not just the job title.

- Identify the new behaviors, skills, and attitudes required.
- Use training plus follow-up support, not a one-shot class.
- Train both skills and attitudes when people have been taught to avoid responsibility.
- Do not underbudget the learning burden.
- Do not make training feel like “shut up and do it this way.”

## 6) Generate short-term wins
Do not hope for wins; plan them.

A real win is:
- visible
- unambiguous
- clearly tied to the change effort

What does not count:
- a good meeting
- a mailed vision statement
- subtle improvements that only insiders can see

Plan wins into budgets and review them on a regular cadence. Clear wins often need to show up within about 6 months in smaller units and within about 18 months in large enterprises.

Use wins to:
- prove sacrifices are worth it
- reward change agents
- weaken cynics and fence-sitters
- keep sponsors engaged
- test and refine the vision against reality

Avoid gimmicks and accounting tricks. Artificial wins can create cynicism and ethical risk.

## 7) Consolidate gains and produce more change
Do not declare victory early.

Short-term wins are a signal to accelerate, not relax. If urgency drops too soon, old forces come back quickly.

Use wins to:
- remove more obstacles
- launch the next wave of change
- eliminate unnecessary approvals, reports, and interdependencies
- delegate more work downward
- keep the coalition active

## 8) Anchor the new approach in culture
Culture comes last. If you try to anchor it before the change is producing results, you will anchor a slogan, not a habit.

To make the new way stick:
- show the performance link between new behavior and better results
- explain why the old culture once helped and why it no longer fits
- reinforce the new way through hiring, promotion, and succession
- use leadership transitions carefully; one bad successor can undo years of work

The new approach is anchored when it becomes “how we do things here.”

## Quick diagnostic questions
- Is there enough urgency to overcome comfort?
- Does the coalition have real power, trust, and central players on board?
- Is the vision simple, believable, and motivating?
- Are leaders repeating it with words and deeds?
- What is blocking action right now?
- Are there visible wins on the calendar?
- Are you accelerating after wins, or relaxing too soon?
- What in hiring, promotion, or succession would cause the change to decay?

## Common failure modes to avoid
- too much complacency
- a weak guiding coalition
- underestimating the vision
- undercommunicating the vision
- leaving obstacles in place
- no short-term wins
- declaring victory too soon
- failing to anchor in culture
